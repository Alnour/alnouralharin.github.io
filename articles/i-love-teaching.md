I love teaching, and I've done it wherever I could for as long as I can remember. From explaining math to classmates in primary school to leading technical workshops years later, I've always enjoyed simplifying complex ideas, building analogies, and watching understanding click.

Two teaching experiences at Oracle stand out as moments I'm especially proud of. The first was a large scale, multi session training initiative on the basics of AI for software engineers in Oracle SaaS. The initiative was led by the distinguished data scientist Anna Morav, a pioneer across multiple domains, especially in time series analysis and, more recently, agentic AI. She set the overall direction and led the core curriculum design. I contributed as a close collaborator by owning a meaningful portion of the content design and co delivering the material alongside the rest of the team. The workshop reached more than 500 attendees across engineers, senior architects, directors, and senior directors. While the size and seniority of the audience were initially intimidating, I reminded myself that the goal remains the same regardless of scale: explain ideas clearly and make them useful. At the same time, I have to be honest, delivering content at that level and to an audience of that size was not easy for me.

Because the sessions were online, measuring engagement required different signals than in person. I paid close attention to attendance trends as a proxy for interest, and seeing participation steadily grow throughout the sessions was deeply motivating. For a teacher, that kind of feedback is incredibly rewarding.

The second experience was a session on agentic coding and its effective use in observability, delivered in collaboration with the distinguished architect Tony Choe. By agentic coding, I mean working with AI systems that can plan, reason, iterate, and write code with a degree of autonomy, acting less like passive tools and more like collaborative teammates. Over the past year, I had been exploring this space deeply through personal research projects using my AI agents research setup, experimenting with how coding agents behave, where they excel, and where they break down.

At that point, I had managed to get coding agents to work continuously for about 25 minutes without interruption, which felt like a major milestone at the time. That was roughly four months ago, and in hindsight it already feels almost prehistoric, given how fast things have been moving. Those early experiments and initial findings shaped much of the session content, some of them genuinely surprising and honestly a bit funny, and I plan to speak more about that work later.

Tony has a rare ability to connect different parts of complex systems, much like a programmer writes code, solving architectural problems with speed and clarity. He also has a deep intuition for the kind of knowledge engineers actually need, which helped us shape outlines that were practical, useful, and immediately actionable. Together, we focused on translating experimentation and research into guidance that engineers could apply right away in real observability workflows.

After sharing the recordings with a broader audience, I received positive feedback, including thoughtful notes from a Group Vice President at Oracle. That recognition further reinforced something I already knew: teaching is a core part of what energizes me, and it is something I intend to keep doing.
