The way most papers are written never really matched how I think.

Most papers assume a fairly linear reader. You start at the abstract, move through the introduction, follow the related work, digest the method, and only then arrive at results and discussion. That structure is clean, logical, and optimized for archival clarity. It is not optimized for how I, and I suspect many others, actually think while learning.

When I read a paper, I feel like I'm pulled by two opposing forces.

The first is a non-linear mindset. I usually start with a set of questions rather than a desire to read the paper end to end. I want to know what problem is being solved, what assumptions are being made, and whether the core idea is even relevant to what I care about. As I try to trace these questions through the paper, I keep running into concepts that are unfamiliar or only partially familiar.

This creates a constant decision point. Do I accept this concept as a temporary building block and keep going, hoping it will make sense once I see the full picture? Or do I stop and look it up properly? The second option is dangerous. One concept leads to another, which leads to another, and before you know it, you've drifted far away from the original paper. It's not unusual to start from a reinforcement learning paper and end up reading something about economics, optimization theory, or cognitive science. Interesting, but not always productive.

The second force is my engineering mindset. This part of my brain is much less patient. It wants concrete answers. Where can this actually be used? What problem does it solve better than existing approaches? Why did the authors choose this method instead of something that seems simpler or more obvious? What are the bottlenecks, the hidden assumptions, the parts that would likely break in practice?

Traditional paper reading often forces these two mindsets to fight each other. The non-linear side wants to jump around, skip details, and follow curiosity. The engineering side wants clarity, trade-offs, and motivation. The paper format doesn't adapt; the reader has to.

For a long time, this tension made reading papers slow and mentally expensive. Not because the material was bad, but because the interface between the paper and my way of thinking was inefficient.

That problem is mostly solved now.

I read papers with a new companion: Gemini, or your preferred LLM, or even your own agentic workflow. The key change is that I make my agenda explicit before or during reading. Instead of adapting myself to the paper, I adapt the paper to my current goal.

I might say:

"Given this paper, explain the core idea assuming I don't care about formal proofs."

"List the main assumptions and where they might fail."

"Compare this method to the most obvious baseline and explain why the authors didn't choose it."

"Summarize this paper as if I want to implement it next week."

This shifts the role of the paper. It's no longer something I passively consume from top to bottom. It becomes a knowledge source that I query.

The result is not depth in the traditional sense. I'm not reading every equation, every appendix, or every experimental detail. But that level of depth is rarely needed upfront. What I get instead is fast orientation. I can understand what a paper is about, why it exists, and whether it deserves deeper attention.

Because of this, I can go through tens of papers a day. Most of them never need a second pass. A small subset earns a deeper, slower read, sometimes even printed out. The rest serve their purpose by giving me context, intuition, and boundaries.

It feels like having an instant, on-demand literature review in your head, shaped exactly the way you want it. The bottleneck is no longer reading speed or cognitive load, but curiosity and intent.

This also changes how I think about the research ecosystem itself. Papers are starting to feel like songs. You don't deeply analyze every song you hear. Most of them give you a vibe, a theme, or a moment. A few stay with you and deserve repeated listening.

Paper-generation factories (like Stanford) start to feel like bands. They produce a steady stream of work with a recognizable style, recurring motifs, and occasional breakout hits. You don't need to obsess over every release to appreciate the landscape.

None of this replaces careful thinking or deep understanding. It just changes when and where you invest that effort.

Interesting times to be learning.
